The number above is for clean sheet aluminum. Aluminum scrap pricing varies more by **grade** than most other metals because the alloy mix and contamination level dramatically affect what a smelter can do with it.

## Common aluminum grades and typical spreads

| Grade | Description | Typical % of sheet |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sheet (clean) | Mill-grade sheet, no paint or coating | 100% (reference) |
| Extrusion | Window frames, structural extrusion | 90–100% |
| Cast | Wheels, engine blocks, cookware | 70–85% |
| Cans (UBC) | Used beverage cans, baled | 60–75% |
| Mixed | Unsorted aluminum scrap | 40–60% |
| Painted / coated | Painted siding, anodized | 50–70% |

## What moves aluminum prices

- **Energy prices** — primary aluminum production is extremely energy-intensive; secondary (recycled) production benefits when energy is expensive
- **Auto demand** — aluminum is increasingly used in vehicle bodies and EV battery housings
- **Beverage can demand** — UBC pricing tracks consumer beverage volumes
- **Bauxite supply** — primary feedstock; supply disruptions push secondary up

## Selling tips

- **Clean sheet pays the most** — strip paint and labels where economic
- **Don't mix cast with sheet** — entire load defaults to cast pricing
- **Cans pay best in volume** — small-volume can sales are barely worth the trip
