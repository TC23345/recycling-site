## Precious Basics

> **Page brief.** Target keyword: `precious metals basics`. Audience: first-time sellers, anyone unfamiliar with the units and conventions. Funnel stage: awareness. The page should answer: *what's a "precious metal", what units are they sold in, and what conventions should I know before I take material to a buyer?*

The barrier to selling precious metals well isn't gold knowledge — it's **unit and convention literacy**. This page covers the foundational vocabulary that buyers assume you already know.

## What counts as "precious"

Five metals are conventionally precious: **gold, silver, platinum, palladium**, and **rhodium**. They share three properties:

- **High value-per-mass** (rhodium and platinum can run thousands per troy ounce)
- **Refining markets exist worldwide** with tight spreads to spot
- **Investment demand** moves prices alongside physical-use demand

Two more — **iridium** and **ruthenium** — round out the platinum-group metals (PGMs) and trade in the same circles, but you almost never encounter them outside industrial settings.

## Troy ounce vs avoirdupois ounce

The single most-confusing convention.

| Unit | Mass | Where you see it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Troy ounce (toz)** | ~31.103 g | Precious metals, gunpowder |
| **Avoirdupois ounce (oz)** | ~28.349 g | Groceries, body weight, mail postage |

When a buyer says "ounce," **always confirm troy or avoirdupois.** The 9% gap matters at $2,500/toz gold. International buyers and most grocery scales use the smaller avoirdupois ounce by default; precious-metal buyers always mean troy.

## Fineness vs karat

Two different ways to express purity.

- **Fineness** is the parts-per-thousand of pure metal (`.999`, `.925`, `.583`). Used universally for silver, platinum, and palladium.
- **Karat** is the parts-per-24 for gold (`24K`, `18K`, `14K`). 24K = pure; 14K = 14/24 = 58.3% gold = .583 fineness.

The two systems cross-reference cleanly:

| Karat | Fineness | % gold |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 24K | .999 | 99.9% |
| 22K | .916 | 91.6% |
| 18K | .750 | 75.0% |
| 14K | .583 | 58.3% |
| 10K | .417 | 41.7% |
| 9K (UK) | .375 | 37.5% |

## Weight, purity, payout — the math

Every precious-metal sale boils down to:

```
payout = weight_in_toz × purity × spot × payout_factor
```

Example: a 14g 14K gold ring, with spot at $2,500/toz, sold to a buyer paying 92% of melt:

- Weight: 14g ÷ 31.103 = 0.450 toz
- Purity: .583
- Gross melt value: 0.450 × .583 × $2,500 = **$655.88**
- Payout: $655.88 × 0.92 = **$603.41**

Master the formula and you can sanity-check any quote in 10 seconds.

## Hallmarks and stamps to look for

Most jewelry is stamped somewhere — clasp, inside band, base of the piece. Common marks:

- **`750`, `18K`, `18KT`** — 18 karat gold
- **`585`, `14K`, `14KT`** — 14 karat gold
- **`925`, `STERLING`, `STER`** — sterling silver
- **`950 PLAT`, `PT950`, `PT900`** — platinum (very high value if confirmed)
- **`PD950`** — palladium (rare, valuable)

Marks that **disqualify** a piece from precious-metal sale:

- **`EPNS`, `EPBM`, `SILVERPLATE`** — electroplated, no melt value
- **`GP`, `GE`, `GF`, `RGP`** — gold-plated, gold electroplate, gold-filled, rolled gold plate; trace gold only
- **`HGE`, `HGP`** — heavy gold electroplate; still trace
- No stamp at all — assume base metal until proven otherwise

## Frequently asked questions

**Is "925" automatically real silver?**

The mark *claims* sterling, but counterfeit hallmarks exist. A magnet test (silver is non-magnetic) catches most plated steel; an acid test ($15 kit) confirms the rest. For high-value pieces, a refiner's XRF scan is definitive.

**Why is platinum worth less than gold sometimes?**

Platinum supply is heavily industrial (auto catalysts), so when global auto production slows, platinum can drift below gold. Historically platinum traded at a premium; for the past several years it's been at a discount.

**My pieces have stones — does that affect the weight?**

Yes. Stones get weighed and subtracted from the gross weight ("stone-weight discount"). Get the discount in writing before the sale.
