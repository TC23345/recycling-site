## Grades & Purity

> **Page brief.** Target keyword: `gold purity karat marks` / `silver purity sterling`. Audience: people identifying inherited or unsorted material before a sale. Funnel stage: consideration. The page should answer: *what does this stamp mean, and what's it worth?*

A complete reference for the purity marks you'll actually encounter on jewelry, coins, and silverware. Cross-reference any stamp against the tables below before you take material to a buyer.

## Gold marks (karat ladder)

| Mark | Fineness | Common region | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `999`, `999.9`, `24K`, `24KT` | .999+ | Bullion / Asia | Pure gold; soft, rare in jewelry except wedding bands |
| `916`, `22K`, `22KT` | .916 | India / UK / South Africa | Krugerrand, Sovereign, Indian wedding jewelry |
| `750`, `18K`, `18KT` | .750 | Europe / luxury | High-end watches, designer rings |
| `585`, `14K`, `14KT` | .585 | US / Canada | US wedding bands, mid-market jewelry — most common in US scrap |
| `417`, `10K`, `10KT` | .417 | US (mass-market) | Class rings, costume jewelry |
| `375`, `9K` | .375 | UK (mass-market) | UK costume / promotional jewelry |
| `333`, `8K` | .333 | Germany | German imports (uncommon in US) |

**Marks that are NOT scrap gold:**

| Mark | What it actually is |
| --- | --- |
| `GP`, `GE`, `GEP` | Gold electroplated — micron-thin layer over base |
| `GF`, `1/20`, `1/10` | Gold-filled — slightly thicker; still trace value, generally rejected |
| `RGP`, `HGE`, `HGP` | Rolled gold plate / heavy gold electroplate — still negligible |
| `KP` (sometimes) | Karat-plumb — actually a *real* karat mark; means "exactly that karat" |
| Vermeil / silver-gilt | Sterling silver with thin gold layer — sells as silver, not gold |

## Silver marks

| Mark | Fineness | What it is |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `999`, `999.9`, `FINE` | .999+ | Modern bullion (Eagle, Maple, generic rounds) |
| `958`, `BRITANNIA` | .958 | UK Britannia coins, premium flatware |
| `925`, `STERLING`, `STER` | .925 | Most sterling jewelry and silverware |
| `900`, `COIN` | .900 | US pre-1965 dimes/quarters/halves; "junk silver" |
| `800` | .800 | Pre-WWII European, some flatware |
| `Mexican silver` (no mark, Mexico) | .720–.925 | Test before assuming |

**Marks that are NOT scrap silver:**

| Mark | What it actually is |
| --- | --- |
| `EPNS` | Electroplated nickel silver — base nickel, plated |
| `EPBM` | Electroplated Britannia metal — same idea |
| `SILVERPLATE`, `SP`, `A1`, `Sheffield` | Plated, base metal underneath |
| `GERMAN SILVER`, `NICKEL SILVER` | No silver content, copper-zinc-nickel alloy |
| `ALPACA` | Same as nickel silver — no silver |

## Platinum marks

| Mark | Fineness | What it is |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `PT1000`, `PLAT`, `PT999` | .999 | Pure platinum — bullion, premium jewelry |
| `PT950`, `950 PLAT` | .950 | Most platinum jewelry |
| `PT900`, `900 PLAT` | .900 | Some Japanese platinum jewelry |
| `IRIDPLAT` | .900 platinum + iridium | Vintage platinum + iridium alloys |

Platinum jewelry is uncommon in scrap lots but very high-value when you find it. Always test — bright white-metal jewelry is more often white gold (alloy of gold and palladium/nickel/silver) than platinum.

## How to identify counterfeit marks

Real precious-metal marks are usually:

- **Crisp and well-formed** — counterfeit stamps are often shallow or slightly off-center
- **Accompanied by maker's marks** — high-value pieces almost always have a maker stamp alongside the purity mark
- **Internally consistent** — a "925" mark on something heavily magnetic is wrong; magnets reveal plated steel underneath silver plating

Three-step verification before sale:

1. **Magnet test** — silver / gold / platinum are non-magnetic. Magnetic = plated base metal.
2. **Acid test** — $15 kit; nitric acid drops by karat. Won't false-positive a good plate.
3. **Buyer XRF scan** — most refiners and serious coin shops have an X-ray fluorescence gun that reads alloy composition non-destructively. Insist on this for any valuable piece.

## Frequently asked questions

**A piece has no mark at all — is it scrap?**

Possibly, but assume base metal until tested. Pre-1900 jewelry is often unmarked even when high purity; modern unmarked jewelry is usually plated or alloyed. Test before assuming.

**What about "925 China" or "925 Italy"?**

The country code is the manufacturing origin. The `925` is the purity claim. Both Italian and Chinese sterling are common and legitimate; verify with magnet/acid like any other piece.

**Is "Tiffany" or "Cartier" worth more as scrap?**

The maker's mark *can* add resale value above melt — but only if you sell to a luxury reseller, not a refiner. Refiners buy by weight and purity. A signed Cartier piece sold to a refiner gets you melt value; the same piece sold through The RealReal or a luxury consignment dealer can pay 3–10× melt for the brand premium. Sort signed pieces out before sending anything to scrap.
