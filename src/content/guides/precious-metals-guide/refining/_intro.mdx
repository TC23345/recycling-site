## Refining & Selling

> **Page brief.** Target keyword: `gold refiner` / `silver refiner` / `where to sell gold`. Audience: someone with material in hand, deciding venue. Funnel stage: consideration → conversion. The page should answer: *who buys precious metals, what cut do they take, and which venue is right for what I have?*

There are four kinds of buyers for precious metals. Each pays differently and is right for different situations.

## Buyer venues

| Venue | What they pay | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Coin shop / local dealer** | 85–96% of melt | Small lots ($100–$5,000), same-day cash, junk silver bags |
| **Mail-in refiner** | 92–98% of melt | Large lots ($5,000+), willing to wait 1–2 weeks |
| **Pawn shop** | 30–60% of melt | Emergency cash; rarely the right call for melt |
| **Luxury consignor** | 3–10× melt (for branded pieces) | Tiffany / Cartier / Van Cleef signed pieces |

The right venue depends on **what you have**, **how fast you need cash**, and **whether anything is signed by a notable maker**.

## What refiners actually do

A refiner takes mixed-purity scrap, melts it, assays the resulting bar (X-ray fluorescence and/or fire assay), and credits you for the actual gold/silver content. Their **payout factor** captures:

- **Melt and assay cost** — fixed per lot, ~$50–$200
- **Refining loss** — 0.1–0.5% of mass evaporates or oxidizes
- **Margin** — what they keep
- **Risk** — float between when they pay you and when they sell the refined metal

For a clean lot of pure-stamped 14K, expect 95–98% of melt. For a mixed bag of unsorted unstamped jewelry, expect 80–90%.

## Vetting a refiner (before you ship anything)

A pre-shipment checklist for any mail-in refiner:

- [ ] BBB rating A or A+, **and** registered with your state's secondhand-dealer / precious-metals dealer license
- [ ] Insured shipping included; the **insurance limit** matches your lot value
- [ ] **Photos of every piece** with a ruler and date stamp before shipping
- [ ] **Recorded weights** with a calibrated scale before shipping
- [ ] Written quote turnaround time (typically 24–48 hrs after receipt)
- [ ] **Free return** if you decline the offer — the deal-breaker. If they don't ship it back free, walk
- [ ] No "melt-on-arrival" clauses — they should hold pieces until you accept the quote

Red flags:

- **No XRF reporting** before melt — you have no way to verify purity claims
- **Pressure to accept first offer** — reputable refiners don't pressure
- **Reviews mentioning "they melted before I accepted"** — disqualifying

## What to expect at a coin shop

- **Walk in with sorted material** — separate karats, separate junk silver from sterling, pull out signed pieces
- **The dealer will weigh on calibrated scales** — watch the scale read; ask for the gram weight before any math
- **They'll quote a price** — usually as `$X per gram for [karat]` or `$Y face for junk silver`
- **They check spot live** — most have a Bloomberg-style ticker; if they're quoting from yesterday's spot, point it out
- **Counter-offer is normal** — splitting the difference between their first offer and your math is standard

## Selling jewelry vs coins vs bullion

**Jewelry** sells by weight × purity × payout factor. Refiners and coin shops both buy. Coin shops usually pay slightly less but are faster.

**Junk silver** (US pre-1965 dimes/quarters/halves) sells by **face value × premium-or-discount-to-spot**. Coin shops pay better than refiners because they resell to numismatic / silver-stacker buyers without smelting.

**Bullion** (Eagles, Maples, Krugerrands, generic rounds) sells closest to spot. Coin shops pay 95–99% of spot for clean Eagles; mail-in dealers (APMEX, JM Bullion, etc.) sometimes pay better, but with shipping risk.

**Numismatic coins** (anything pre-1933 US, certified MS-graded coins, key-date coins) should NEVER be melted. Sell to a coin dealer with numismatic specialty, NOT a generalist or refiner.

## Frequently asked questions

**Should I clean my coins before selling?**

**No.** Cleaning destroys numismatic premium. Tarnish on circulated silver coins is part of the wear pattern that buyers expect. A polished silver dollar pays melt; an uncleaned MS-graded one can pay 5–50× melt.

**What documentation do I need?**

Most US states require coin/precious-metal dealers to record seller ID for transactions over a threshold (varies by state, often $500). Bring your driver's license. For mail-in lots, you'll typically sign an affidavit attesting you own the material legitimately.

**Is the price taxable?**

If you sold for more than you paid (or what you inherited it at), the gain is generally taxable as a collectible (28% federal cap for individuals as of recent law). Refiners over a threshold ($600+ historically) issue 1099 forms. Consult a tax professional for anything substantial.
