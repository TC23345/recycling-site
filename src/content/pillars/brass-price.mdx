Brass isn't a directly-traded commodity — it's an alloy of copper (≈65%) and zinc (≈35%) by mass. The price above is **derived from live copper futures** with a typical brass-to-copper ratio applied. Yards quote brass with their own grade and contamination discounts on top.

## Brass grade ladder

| Grade | Description | Typical notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Yellow brass | Most common; plumbing fittings, decorative hardware | Reference grade |
| Red brass | Higher copper content, valves, marine fittings | Pays slightly more than yellow |
| Plumbing brass | Mixed brass with possible solder/chrome | Discounted for cleanup cost |
| Brass shells | Spent ammunition casings | Often a separate counter at the yard |
| Dirty brass | Contaminated with steel or other metal | Discounted heavily |

## What moves brass prices

Brass tracks copper closely because copper is the majority of the alloy by mass and value. Secondary drivers:

- **Zinc spot** — small but real impact on the alloy economics
- **Plumbing demand** — construction cycles drive brass fitting volumes
- **Recycling supply** — brass is highly recyclable; abundant supply caps pricing
- **Specialty applications** — marine, ammunition, decorative arts each have their own demand cycle

## Selling tips

- **Strip steel** from brass items — fittings often have steel inserts that contaminate the load
- **Separate red from yellow** if your yard pays differently for each
- **Keep shells separate** — many yards have a dedicated buying station with documentation requirements
