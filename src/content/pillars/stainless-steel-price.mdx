Stainless steel scrap pricing is **driven by nickel** — 304 stainless contains roughly 8% nickel, and 316 stainless about 10%. When nickel rallies, stainless scrap follows; when nickel collapses, stainless can briefly trade close to carbon-steel prices.

## Stainless grade ladder

| Grade | Composition | Typical use | Pricing notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 304 | ~18% Cr / 8% Ni | Cookware, sinks, food equipment | Most common scrap; reference grade |
| 316 | ~16% Cr / 10% Ni / 2% Mo | Marine, chemical, medical | Pays premium over 304 |
| 430 | ~17% Cr / minimal Ni | Auto trim, decorative | Magnetic; pays much less than 304 |
| Mixed | Unknown alloy | — | Defaults to lowest grade in the load |

## What moves stainless prices

- **Nickel cycle** — the biggest single factor; LME nickel is the watch metric
- **Stainless mill demand** — auto, appliances, construction
- **Indonesian nickel supply** — Indonesia dominates global supply; policy changes ripple immediately
- **EV battery demand** — nickel sulfate competes with stainless feedstock

## Selling tips

- **Magnet test first** — magnetic stainless (430-series) pays a fraction of non-magnetic (304/316)
- **Separate by grade** if you have meaningful 316 — it pays a premium
- **Avoid mixed loads** — yards default to the lowest grade in the pile
